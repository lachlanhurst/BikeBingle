Concept

The basic concept of BikeBingle is to enable cyclists from around the world to log and geo-tag any accidents they have. Over time it is hoped (err, maybe hoped isn't the best word) that a significant database will be built up. As all incidents are geo-tagged users can view the accident history of a specific location.

Such functionality may be of use to a commuter planning a new route as BikeBingle may assist in helping the cyclist to avoid the most accident prone locations. It may also be of benefit to be made aware of specific dangers at a given location, for example a troublesome pothole that has caused a few 'off's', frequent doorings along a stretch of road, and so on.

BikeBingle is not intended to replace formal reporting of bicycle accidents, it is important for the relevant authorities to collect accurate statistics so that action can be taken if deemed necessary. It is hoped that BikeBingle will fill the reporting gap in official channels whereby minor accidents are not reported. This may be a result of several factors; the cyclist involved may not warrent the accident as significant enough to report, or be unaware that they should report such incidents.

Motivation

After my first few bingles (accidents) I though it would be interesting to keep track of where, when, why and how they happened. It was really only done for curiosities sake. This was very easy to do via Google Maps, but did have its limitations, the big one being there was no means to collaborate on a large scale.

The solution was of course to develop BikeBingle.

Implementation

BikeBingle is hosted on the Google App Engine framework, this has served to be an excellent development platform. Furthermore the free bandwidth and CPU usage quotas mean that BikeBingle has cost nothing but time. Big thanks to Google for providing the resources to turn an idea into reality with no capital required.

Given Google's reliability I'm betting the App Engine framework will be supported for a long time to come. BikeBingle is in good hands.

The user interface was created using the Google Web Toolkit. This was great for a guy that could write a Java Swing app in his sleep, but was yet to jump into the world of Web 2.0.

Future Plans

I've got lots, some I really wanted to squeeze into the initial launch of BikeBingle but unfortunately got cut. Instead of dragging out the development timeline I though it best to get something out and see what you, the user, would like to see. So on the Help panel you'll find a feedback button, which is really just a link to the BikeBingle blog where you can leave feedback. Good or bad, it's all appreciated.

On the off chance I do get board you may find some graph functionality appear, BikeBingle gadgets and some view filters (by date range, injury severity, etc). I'd also like to do some documentation of the web-server with the aim that other web apps could be developed to extract information out of the BikeBingle database.
